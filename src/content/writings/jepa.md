For a long time, the default way to teach a model about the world was to make it draw the world. Reconstruction losses, pixel-level video prediction, autoencoders that have to put back every shadow and texture they see. The reasoning was simple. If a model can render the next frame correctly, it must understand the scene. That was the bet, and it powered a decade of representation learning.

The problem with that bet shows up the moment you try to use such a model to actually control a robot. You end up paying the full cost of rendering at every planning step, and most of what you render is irrelevant to the decision at hand. Whether the cup casts a slightly different shadow does not change whether your gripper closes around it. But the model spent capacity learning to predict that shadow anyway, because the loss told it to.

Joint-Embedding Predictive Architectures, or JEPAs, take the opposite bet. Predict the future, but only in latent space, and only the parts of it that are actually predictable. Skip the pixels. Skip the unpredictable noise. What started as a self-supervised image pretraining recipe has, over the past three years, become something stranger and more interesting. It is now a working substrate for zero-shot robot control, fast enough to plan in real time on a single GPU, and as of 2026, understood well enough that the whole family can be described by a single probabilistic framework.

## The four-piece recipe

Every JEPA paper instantiates the same template, which is worth pinning down before anything else. There is an encoder that maps an observation to a latent vector. There is a target encoder, usually an exponential moving average of the first one, that maps the ground-truth future or masked region into its own target latent. There is a predictor that takes the visible latent plus some conditioning (mask tokens, or actions, or both, or a sampled latent variable) and tries to produce the target latent. And there is a loss, which is just a distance in latent space between what the predictor produced and what the target encoder said the answer was.

That is it. No pixel reconstruction. No contrastive negatives. No view invariance objective. No reward signal.

The obvious question is why this does not collapse. A predictor trying to match a target encoder could trivially win by having both sides output a constant. Every JEPA variant is, in some sense, a different answer to that collapse problem. I-JEPA handled it with the EMA target encoder plus an asymmetric architecture between context and target. Point-JEPA and 3D-JEPA ported the idea to point clouds, one with a greedy sequencer, the other with a discrete VAE teacher for richer targets. V-JEPA 2 scaled it to a billion parameters of video with 3D rotary position embeddings. LeWorldModel, in late 2025, replaced the EMA trick entirely with an explicit isotropic Gaussian prior on the latents (called SIGReg), making the whole thing trainable end-to-end on raw pixels with a single hyperparameter. And most recently Var-JEPA, which I will come back to later, showed that the EMA trick, SIGReg, and VICReg-style covariance penalties are all implicit surrogates for the KL terms of a specific variational autoencoder. The anti-collapse mechanisms were never unrelated tricks. They were all groping toward the same objective.

Two axes of innovation, then. The modalities the recipe stretches across (image, point cloud, video, pixel video with actions, tabular), and the way each variant prevents the predictor from giving up and outputting a constant.

## Why predict-only-the-predictable matters

This is where JEPA starts to look genuinely different from everything else. A reconstruction-based latent is forced to encode every pixel, because the loss will punish it for missing details. A JEPA latent is only forced to encode whatever the predictor needs to match the target encoder's view of the future. If a piece of the scene is fundamentally unpredictable, the optimal thing for the encoder to do is throw it away. There is no gradient pushing it to keep noise around.

The consequence shows up vividly in LeWorldModel's "violation of expectation" test. They probe the trained latent's surprise signal under two kinds of perturbation. When you teleport an object across the scene, breaking physics, the surprise spikes. When you change the color of an object, leaving physics intact, the surprise barely moves. That is the opposite bias from a reconstruction-trained latent, which would notice the color change loudly and the teleportation only insofar as the pixels rearranged.

For control, this is the right bias. You want a latent that cares about dynamics and ignores cosmetics. You want planning that reasons about whether the gripper reaches the cup, not whether the lighting is correct.

There is a catch to this argument that took the field a while to spot, and I will return to it when we get to V-JEPA 2.1.

## The control comparison: JEPA against everything else

This is the part I find most interesting, because it is the hardest to see without putting the alternatives side by side.

Consider Vision-Language-Action models first. The Pi-series, SmolVLA, GR00T N1, all of them. These are reactive systems. Observation goes in, action comes out, no imagination involved. They need labeled demonstrations, which means real teleoperation hours from real humans. JEPA-based world models are deliberative instead. They plan by imagining a trajectory in latent space and optimizing the action sequence that gets there, and they pretrain on unlabeled data with no rewards. The headline result here is V-JEPA 2-AC plus 62 hours of unlabeled Droid robot video beating Octo, a VLA fine-tuned on all of Droid, on pick-and-place. Less data, no labels, better results.

Now consider pixel-space video world models. DreamGen, DreamZero, Cosmos. These render frames as part of their planning loop, which is why they work as imagination substrates but cost real wall-clock time to query. V-JEPA 2-AC versus Cosmos on the same hardware comes out at 16 seconds per action versus 4 minutes per action, a 15x speedup. On the pick-and-place cup task, the success rates were 80 percent versus 0 percent. The gap is not that pixel models are bad, it is that they are paying for visual fidelity the controller does not need. JEPA skips that bill entirely. LeWorldModel pushes this even further by compressing the entire observation into a single CLS token, which makes its planner 48x faster than DINO-WM, a competing latent world model that uses around 200 patch tokens per frame.

Then there is Dreamer's RSSM, which is also a latent world model, but trained with a reconstruction loss plus a KL term. Dreamer avoids collapse because it has to put the image back. That is a perfectly reasonable trick, but it ties the latent to all pixel content, including all the appearance detail that will not affect the next decision. Dreamer also needs an environment reward to close its RL loop. JEPA needs neither. The "is this physically plausible" signal comes entirely from how well the predictor can match the target encoder's embeddings, with no decoder and no reward function in sight.

Finally there is VISTA, a hierarchical world model that generates sparse pixel subgoals for a downstream VLA to execute. VISTA's "world model" is really a high-level planner that hands off to a separate controller. JEPA world models are monolithic. The same network is the encoder, the predictor, and the planning target. There is no second model to integrate. The trade-off is real. VISTA scales to harder semantic planning because pixel goals carry rich object-identity information, while V-JEPA 2-AC handles reactive geometric control more gracefully. Different tools, different jobs.

## The four-stage arc

If you read the JEPA papers in order, you can see the program move through four regimes that look almost like product phases.

The first phase, roughly 2023 to 2024, was JEPA as a pure self-supervised backbone. I-JEPA showed that predictive latent SSL could match DINO and iBOT on image classification, beat MAE and data2vec on compute efficiency, and win on low-level vision tasks like counting and depth estimation. Point-JEPA and 3D-JEPA repeated the trick on point clouds, with a greedy sequencer for ordering patches and a context-aware decoder with a dVAE teacher for richer targets. The goal at this stage was just to produce good frozen features for downstream tasks.

The second phase, in 2025, was scaling. V-JEPA 2 took the recipe to a billion parameters and 22 million internet videos, and the resulting frozen encoder turned out to be a strong general visual backbone. State of the art on Something-Something v2, on Epic-Kitchens action anticipation, and competitive on video question answering once aligned with an LLM. At this point JEPA stopped being a niche SSL trick and started being a reasonable alternative to CLIP or DINOv2 for any task that involves understanding motion.

The third phase, also in 2025, is the part that surprised me. V-JEPA 2-AC and LeWorldModel both demonstrated planning directly in JEPA latent space as a deployment-ready substrate. V-JEPA 2-AC does this with massive internet-scale pretraining followed by 62 hours of action-conditioned post-training on Droid, then deploys zero-shot to a Franka arm in unseen labs. LeWorldModel does it from scratch in a few hours on a single GPU, planning in under a second per query. Together they bracket the design space. One end says pretrain massively then specialize. The other end says train end-to-end small and cheap. Both work. That is a bigger deal than it might sound, because it suggests the JEPA recipe is not fragile to scale or to training regime in the way that, say, diffusion-based world models often are.

The fourth phase is 2026, and it is where the design space starts to consolidate rather than expand. Two papers came out within weeks of each other attacking the remaining rough edges from opposite directions. V-JEPA 2.1, from the same Meta FAIR group that built V-JEPA 2, fixed the dense-feature weakness by changing which tokens contribute to the loss. Var-JEPA, from a smaller independent group, showed that the entire JEPA design pattern is a deterministic specialization of a variational autoencoder, which recasts every anti-collapse trick in the literature as an implicit approximation to a principled ELBO objective. These two papers are doing very different things, but they are both symptoms of the same thing. JEPA is stabilizing into a mature paradigm, and the field is starting to understand why it works rather than just that it works.

## The context-token problem, and what fixed it

Here is the catch I flagged earlier. V-JEPA 2's features, for all their strength on global tasks, were noisy at the patch level. If you ran PCA on its dense feature map and looked at the result, you got blurry blobs rather than clean object boundaries. Linear-probe depth estimation on NYUv2 came out at 0.682 RMSE, roughly twice as bad as DINOv3 at 0.309. On semantic segmentation in ADE20K, V-JEPA 2 hit 22 mean intersection-over-union. DINOv3 hit 55. The global tasks did not suffer, but anything that needed precise local structure came apart.

The natural reading of this was that predict-only-the-predictable was eating its own tail. Fine-grained spatial detail is harder to predict than scene-level semantics, so maybe the encoder was learning to discard it. If that were true, dense features would just be the price of the JEPA bias, and you would have to accept a split between JEPA for dynamics and DINO for geometry.

That is not what was happening. The V-JEPA 2.1 paper ran the diagnosis and found a much simpler culprit. The JEPA loss, as written, only supervises the predictor's output for the masked tokens. The context tokens (the visible patches that the encoder saw) are never themselves a prediction target. So the encoder has no incentive to encode local information in them. Instead, it uses them as scratchpad space for aggregating global context about the scene, because that is what actually helps predict the masked regions. Think of it as the encoder unconsciously inventing register tokens, except spread across every patch in the visible input.

Once the problem is diagnosed this way, the fix is almost embarrassingly direct. Apply the same prediction loss to context tokens, not just masked ones. The context tokens now have to individually match the target encoder's view of the corresponding patches, which forces each one to carry real local content. V-JEPA 2.1 does exactly this, with one extra detail that matters. The weight on each context token's loss is inversely proportional to the square root of its distance from the nearest masked region. Tokens near the mask boundary get supervised heavily, tokens far from the mask less so. This weighting avoids a trivial shortcut where the encoder just copies its own context features forward, and it empirically trades off cleanly between dense-task performance and global-task performance.

A second ingredient helps. The prediction loss is applied at four intermediate encoder layers, not just the final one. This is an old trick in representation learning that turns out to matter a lot here, because it forces local information to flow from early layers to late layers rather than being summarized away.

The combined result is what I would have bet against two years ago. V-JEPA 2.1, at 2 billion parameters, hits 0.307 RMSE on NYUv2 depth, which edges out DINOv3 at 7 billion parameters. On semantic segmentation it gets 85 on Pascal VOC and 48 on ADE20K, close enough to the best image encoders that the video substrate stops being a handicap. And critically, the global tasks that V-JEPA 2 was already strong on did not regress. SSv2 action classification went from 77.3 percent to 77.7 percent, a new state of the art.

What this tells us about the appearance-versus-dynamics question is important. The old reading was that JEPA's bias was structurally at odds with dense geometric understanding. The new reading is that JEPA can encode dense information just fine, as long as you actually ask it to. The loss design, not the fundamental predict-only-the-predictable principle, was what was costing the field. Reconstruction was never actually needed.

And this matters concretely for robotics. Running V-JEPA 2.1 through the same action-conditioned post-training recipe as V-JEPA 2-AC gives you a 20 percent improvement in Franka grasp success rate out of the box, without any changes to the planner or the action-conditioning module. The dense depth understanding shows up as better grasps. On robot navigation, plugging V-JEPA 2.1 representations into the NWM planning framework gives a 10x speedup (10.6 seconds per plan instead of 103 seconds) while also improving trajectory accuracy. Better representations, same everything else.

## Everything was secretly a VAE

The other 2026 paper comes at JEPA from a completely different angle, and it is the one that changed how I think about the family as a whole.

Here is the setup. You have a context observation x, a target observation y, and you want to learn latents s_x and s_y plus an auxiliary variable z that captures what is predictable about s_y from s_x. The standard JEPA architecture gives you an encoder for s_x, a target encoder for s_y (typically an EMA copy), and a predictor that maps s_x to s_y via z. That looks like three deterministic neural networks wired together with a stop-gradient.

But if you replace each of those deterministic mappings with a Gaussian conditional distribution, something interesting happens. The context encoder becomes the variational posterior q(s_x | x). The target encoder becomes the variational posterior q(s_y | s_x, z, y). The predictor becomes, specifically, a learned conditional prior p(s_y | s_x, z). Add a decoder for x and a decoder for y, apply the standard VAE recipe, derive the evidence lower bound, and out pops an objective that looks almost exactly like the JEPA loss plus a few KL terms.

This is the Var-JEPA contribution. They reverse-engineered the probabilistic model whose ELBO reproduces the JEPA architecture, and then they actually trained it. The five loss terms are interpretable. Two are reconstruction terms (recovering x from s_x and y from s_y). Two are KL terms pulling s_x and z toward standard Gaussian priors. One is a KL term pulling the target posterior toward the learned conditional prior, which is the actual predictor.

The practical payoff is that collapse prevention comes for free. The KL terms prevent the encoders from outputting constants, because a constant output has infinite KL to the prior. No EMA, no stop-gradient, no SIGReg, no VICReg. Ablations show this holds up. Remove the KL terms and the representation collapses. Remove the reconstruction terms and the probe accuracy drops to chance. Each piece of the ELBO is doing principled work.

There is a second consequence, which is arguably more interesting. The auxiliary variable z, which every JEPA paper since 2022 has had in its diagrams but which none of them actually sampled from, becomes a real reparameterized Gaussian latent in Var-JEPA. When you run the model at inference time, you can sample z to get different plausible predictions of s_y. That is the original LeCun vision of using a latent variable to represent multi-modal futures, and Var-JEPA is the first paper to actually operationalize it. Every prior JEPA handled multi-modality implicitly, by letting the target encoder be invariant to unpredictable variation. Var-JEPA handles it explicitly, by putting the variability in z.

A third consequence is uncertainty quantification. Because the context encoder outputs a full Gaussian posterior rather than a point estimate, the covariance gives you a per-sample confidence score. Var-JEPA shows that abstaining on the most-uncertain 10 to 50 percent of inputs gives monotonic accuracy gains across tabular benchmarks. That is a capability no other JEPA has.

The big caveat is that Var-JEPA has so far only been instantiated on tabular data. Scaling the ELBO to V-JEPA 2's regime of 2 billion parameters and 22 million videos is the obvious next step, and it is not obvious that it will just work. But the theoretical contribution stands regardless. JEPA is not a parallel, non-generative alternative to likelihood-based SSL. It is a specific deterministic limit of a particular variational model, and the entire family's accumulated anti-collapse tricks are implicit approximations to a principled objective that has been sitting there the whole time.

## A sixth world-model paradigm

The earlier writeup on world models in this series listed five paradigms for using a world model in robotics. Compact latent world models like Dreamer. Video world models as real-time policies, like WAM. Video world models as virtual RL environments. Video world models as sparse subgoal planners, like VISTA. And video world models as synthetic data factories, like DreamGen.

JEPA, taken together, adds a sixth. Call it self-supervised latent world model as MPC substrate. The distinguishing features are concrete. It trains from observation and action only, with no reward. Its rollout happens entirely in latent space, with planning done by something like cross-entropy method or gradient-based action search at test time. It never generates pixels at any stage of training or deployment. And it scales gracefully across roughly two orders of magnitude in parameter count, from LeWM at 15 million to V-JEPA 2.1 at 2 billion, with the same core recipe.

That last property is what makes it feel like a genuine paradigm rather than a single point on the design space. The recipe does not break when you change the budget. It just gives you proportionally better or proportionally cheaper models.

## What is still unsolved

Long-horizon rollout is still the most visible wall, though it has moved somewhat. V-JEPA 2 degraded when you asked it to plan over 8-step horizons. V-JEPA 2.1 actually improves at horizon 8, because its dense features let the predictor reason about fine-grained spatial structure across longer trajectories. So the wall has shifted from "2 or 3 steps before drift" to "8 or more steps before drift," which is a real change, but it is still nowhere near what humans do when they plan minutes ahead.

The proposed architectural answer for this is Hierarchical JEPA (H-JEPA), which stacks JEPAs at different abstraction levels so each predictor handles a different time horizon. LeCun sketched it in the 2022 position paper, and MC-JEPA in 2023 built a sort of proto-H-JEPA for optical flow via a multi-resolution predictor. But no one has built H-JEPA for long-horizon latent rollout at anything like meaningful scale. The progressive-resolution training in V-JEPA 2 (which fine-tunes on 64-frame clips after pretraining on 16-frame clips) is a curriculum-based approximation, not a real hierarchy. Actually making H-JEPA work, with latent sampling at the abstract level feeding goals to the concrete level, is the most important open problem in the program.

Scaling Var-JEPA to video is the second open problem. The theoretical unification is compelling, but the actual model has only been tested on Adult, Covertype, and MNIST. Whether the ELBO stays stable at a billion parameters with 16-frame 256-pixel video clips is the next experiment. If it does, you get single-hyperparameter collapse prevention, real latent-variable sampling, and per-sample uncertainty all at once, which is a genuinely different animal than current V-JEPA 2.1.

Action abstraction is another sticking point. Every action-conditioned JEPA today uses 7-DoF end-effector deltas, because that is what Droid logs and what Franka arms accept. Dexterous hands with 16 or more degrees of freedom, whole-body humanoid control with hundreds, and navigation with continuous steering, none of these have been seriously touched by the JEPA control line yet. V-JEPA 2.1's navigation results are a start, but only in 3-DoF (translation and yaw). Whether the predict-only-the-predictable bias still pays off when the action space is much higher-dimensional is genuinely unclear.

And finally, there is the question of what other boxes from LeCun's 2022 position paper actually get built. JEPA, however it ends up stabilizing, is only one module (the world model) in a seven-module cognitive architecture that also includes a configurator, a trainable critic, a short-term memory system, an intrinsic cost system, and a two-mode actor. V-JEPA 2-AC's CEM planner is a rough approximation of the Mode 2 actor, but none of the other modules exist. The program around JEPA is further along than the program around, say, the configurator. That is an architectural gap, and it matters for how you read progress. Having a world module that works is a necessary condition for the full vision, not a sufficient one.

## Why this changes the substrate

For years, the implicit choice in physical AI was between two unappetizing options. You could train a reactive policy from labeled demonstrations and hope it generalized, or you could train a generative video model and pay enormous inference cost to use it for planning. JEPA makes a third option viable. Train an unlabeled latent predictor from internet video and robot logs, then plan inside it cheaply at deployment time. Skip the demonstrations, skip the rendering, skip the reward shaping.

The numbers back this up in concrete ways that matter for practitioners. Sixteen seconds per action instead of four minutes. Eighty percent grasp success instead of zero, and with V-JEPA 2.1 pushing that toward 100. A planning latent that ignores irrelevant color changes and notices physics violations. Dense features that match DINOv3 on depth estimation without giving up video-scale dynamics understanding. A recipe that scales from a single-GPU prototype to a two-billion-parameter foundation model without changing shape. And, as of 2026, a unified theoretical account that explains why the accumulated anti-collapse tricks worked and shows how to derive them from first principles.

The bet behind reconstruction-based world models was that drawing the world is the same as understanding it. JEPA is the strongest evidence so far that those two things come apart, and that for control specifically, it is understanding without drawing that you actually want. The field spent a decade painting pixels to understand scenes. It turns out you can do the understanding first, and paint only when someone asks you to.
